How to Choose a Reliable Indian Agricultural Exporter: A Buyer's Due Diligence Checklist

The Indian agro export industry has thousands of legitimate professional exporters — and a smaller but significant number of unreliable operators targeting first-time international buyers. The difference between them is easily visible if you know what to check.

Step 1: Verify Basic Legal Registration (15 minutes)

Every legitimate Indian agro exporter must have these — all verifiable online:

DocumentHow to VerifyRed Flag
IEC (Import Export Code)dgft.gov.in → IEC searchNo IEC = cannot legally export
APEDA RCMCapeda.gov.in → member searchNo APEDA = cannot export rice, spices, fruits
GSTINgst.gov.in → taxpayer searchNo GSTIN = not a registered company in India
Company registrationmca.gov.in → company searchNo MCA = potentially not a legal entity

Step 2: Assess Export Track Record

  • APEDA export data: Ask supplier for their APEDA export statistics — volume of exports = track record
  • Bank reference letter: A reputable exporter's bank issues a reference letter confirming trade finance relationship
  • Existing customer references: Ask for 2–3 international buyer references and contact them directly
  • LinkedIn / Google presence: Legitimate exporters have a professional company page and searchable presence

Red Flags That Signal a High-Risk Supplier

  • Refuses to show IEC, APEDA RCMC, or GSTIN
  • Payment must go to an individual's personal bank account (not company account)
  • Prices significantly below all other quotes — unrealistically cheap
  • Cannot provide physical samples within 48 hours for a product they claim to stock
  • Communication only via WhatsApp with no company email domain
  • APEDA data shows very low or zero exports despite claiming large volumes

Step 3: Protect Your First Shipment

  • Use LC (Letter of Credit) rather than TT until 2–3 successful transactions completed
  • Insist on SGS or Bureau Veritas loading inspection
  • Request pre-shipment lab CoA for pesticide MRLs, mycotoxins, and heavy metals
  • Keep first order to 1–2 containers maximum — test before scaling
  • Get marine cargo insurance covering CIF value + 10%
